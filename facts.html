Facts About the Royal Bengal Tiger

  • The Royal Bengal Tiger is the national animal of both India and Bangladesh.
  • The Bengal tiger's coat is yellow to light orange, with stripes ranging from dark brown to black; the belly and the interior parts of the limbs are white, and the tail is orange with black rings.

    • A Bengal Tiger in Kanha National Park, Madhya Pradesh

  • The Bengal Tiger's main diet consists of gaur, sambar, chital, barasingha, water buffalo, nilgai, serow, takin, wild boar, hog deer, muntjac and the northern plains gray langur. Although,
    there are rare cases where the tiger has hunted Indian leopards, mugger crocodiles, Asian black bears, sloth bears, and dhole. Very few cases of tigers hunting King cobras, Indian elephants and
    Indian rhinoceroses have occured

    • A Bengal Tiger with its prey at Ranthambore.

  • Unlike most animals, Bengal Tigers do not have a defined mating season and mate throughout the year.

    • A Bengal Tiger with its cubs in Maharashtra.

  • Due to an utter faliure by the Indian, Bangladeshi, and Nepali governments, poaching of the Bengal Tiger thrives in these areas, leading to the Bengal Tiger being classified as "endangered". However, conservation efforts in these countries over the last 50 years have led to small increases in population.
  • The Bengal Tiger serves as a vital role of ancient Indian mythology, appearing on the coins of the South Indian dynasties Cholas, Cheras and the Pandyas. It also appears on the Indus-Valley
    era divine seal of Shri Pashupatinath, none other than the incarnation of Lord Shiva himself.

    • The Tiger on Shri Pashupati's divine seal, found in Mohenjodaro

  • One of the most famous Bengal Tiger is the "Demon of Champawat" (1895-1907), a female tigress living in modern-day Uttarakhand and Nepal which killed 436 people. It was shot and killed
    by the infamed Jim Corbett, which eventually led to his rise to fame.

    • One of the last remaining photographs of the Champawati Tiger

    Mutated Bengal Tigers

  • A special recessive mutant of the Bengal Tiger commonly found in the wild in Madhya Pradesh, Assam, Bengal and Bihar is the White Tiger, due to the lack of red and yellow pheomelanin pigments.

    • A White Bengal Tiger in Singapore

  • A white tiger with an excessive amount of melanin results in a Pseudo-Melanistic White Tiger

    • A Pseudo-Melanistic White Tiger in Nandankanan Park, Odisha

  • A mutated white tiger can result in it having no stripes, making it a pure white stripeless tiger.

    • A Stripeless White Tiger in Las Vegas
